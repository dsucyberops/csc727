## Why These Five Criteria? (from §4.2)

The five evaluation dimensions were selected to capture performance considerations relevant to practitioners and policymakers — not merely academic benchmarkers. Each criterion answers a distinct operational question that a grader, regulator, or platform engineer would actually need answered:

| Criterion | Operational question answered | Primary source motivating inclusion |
|---|---|---|
| Benchmark accuracy | What does the best-case controlled evaluation report? | Standard metric; baseline needed for comparison |
| Generalization | Will this still work on content from architectures not in training data? | Soundarya & Gururaj (2026); Gong & Li (2024); Lin et al. (2025) |
| Adversarial robustness | Will this hold when the attacker optimizes specifically to evade it? | Khan et al. (2025) |
| Computational cost | Can typical platform infrastructure run this at scale? | Singh et al. (2025) |
| Real-time deployment feasibility | Can this realistically be mandated for content moderation? | Lin et al. (2025); Singh et al. (2025) |

The first criterion reflects current academic evaluation practice. The remaining four reflect gaps between academic evaluation and operational reality documented in the reviewed literature. A detection method scoring well on benchmark accuracy but poorly on generalization, robustness, or deployability creates false assurance — which is worse than no detection claim at all, because it prevents organizations from seeking alternative protections.

## Criterion Definitions and Rating Scales

### 1. Benchmark Accuracy
**Definition:** Performance on standard deepfake detection benchmark datasets as reported in primary sources.  
**Critical caveat (Gong & Li, 2024):** Benchmark datasets are constructed under controlled conditions that systematically inflate accuracy. These figures are upper bounds on operational performance.  
**Scale:** Very Low (<60%) | Low (60–70%) | Moderate (70–80%) | Moderate-High (80–88%) | High (88–94%) | Highest (>94%)

### 2. Generalization
**Definition:** Ability to correctly classify deepfakes generated by architectures not in training data. Primary test: GAN-trained detectors on diffusion content.  
**Why it matters:** Soundarya & Gururaj (2026) show GAN-trained models fail against diffusion content. Gong & Li (2024) show benchmarks don't include out-of-distribution examples. Lin et al. (2025) confirm real-world degradation. A detector optimized for today's content faces out-of-distribution inputs within months.  
**Scale:** Low (<45%) | Moderate (45–68%) | Moderate-High (68–78%) | High (>78%)

### 3. Adversarial Robustness
**Definition:** Resistance when the generative model is optimized specifically to defeat the detector. Distinct from generalization: a model can be adversarially robust within its training distribution while still failing on new architectures.  
**Source:** Khan et al. (2025) — no single method is robust across all modalities and adversarial conditions.  
**Scale:** Very Low | Low | Low-Moderate | Moderate | Moderate-High | High

### 4. Computational Cost
**Definition:** Relative compute requirements for inference at scale.  
**Why it matters:** Singh et al. (2025) identify this as the primary barrier to multimodal ensemble deployment. Cost determines platform-scale feasibility.  
**Scale:** Very Low | Low | Moderate | High

### 5. Real-Time Deployment Feasibility
**Definition:** Can this realistically be deployed in a platform content moderation pipeline right now?  
**Values:** Yes (standard hardware sufficient) | Partial (GPU required; not CPU-viable) | No (dedicated infrastructure investment required)

## Per-Method Rating Justification

### Visual Artifact Detection
| Criterion | Rating | Source & Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Benchmark accuracy | High (~92%) | Multiple GAN-era benchmarks (FaceForensics++, DFDC) report >90%; Guarnera et al. (2023) |
| Generalization | Low (~38%) | Near-chance on diffusion content per Guarnera et al. (2023); mechanism explained by Liu et al. (2025): diffusion produces architecturally different spatial artifacts |
| Adversarial robustness | Low | Trivially bypassed by post-processing (Gaussian blur, JPEG re-compression) or adversarial generator training |
| Compute | Low | Single-pass CNN inference; minimal latency |
| Real-time | Yes | Widely deployed in commercial content moderation |

**Summary:** High deployability and GAN accuracy, but structurally unreliable as the generation paradigm shifts. Not a viable primary strategy going forward.

---

### Biological Signal Analysis
| Criterion | Rating | Source & Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Benchmark accuracy | Moderate (~78%) | Khan et al. (2025) report moderate accuracy range |
| Generalization | Moderate (~62%) | More architecture-agnostic than artifact methods — targets physiological patterns rather than generation-specific artifacts. Degrades when high-fps source video allows generative models to simulate biological timing |
| Adversarial robustness | Moderate | Requires attacker to simulate biological signals — adds cost but achievable |
| Compute | Low | Temporal signal extraction computationally light |
| Real-time | Yes | Feasible in video stream analysis |

---

### Audio Spectral Analysis
| Criterion | Rating | Source & Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Benchmark accuracy | Moderate-High (~84%) | Martinek (2025); Singh et al. (2025) report moderate-high on benchmark audio |
| Generalization | Moderate (~58%) | Newer voice cloning architectures (VITS, VALL-E) substantially close the spectral gap. Particularly weak on short clips (<3s) — exactly where most real-world BEC voice clips fall (§5.1) |
| Adversarial robustness | Low-Moderate | Spectral post-processing can partially mask artifacts |
| Compute | Low | FFT-based analysis is computationally efficient |
| Real-time | Yes | Real-time audio stream analysis feasible |

---

### Metadata Analysis
| Criterion | Rating | Source & Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Benchmark accuracy | Moderate (~71%) | Lin et al. (2025) evaluate in controlled (metadata-intact) conditions |
| Generalization | Low (~32%) | Metadata stripping is trivial and costs the attacker nothing. Generalization assumes metadata survives distribution — an assumption that fails reliably in adversarial contexts |
| Adversarial robustness | Very Low | Zero robustness against stripping or spoofing — a trivial operation |
| Compute | Very Low | Reading file headers requires negligible computation |
| Real-time | Yes | Near-instantaneous; useful only as first-pass filter |

---

### ML-based Classifiers
| Criterion | Rating | Source & Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Benchmark accuracy | High (~91%) | Khan et al. (2025) report high accuracy on matching-distribution test sets |
| Generalization | Moderate (~66%) | Better than artifact methods but limited by training distribution; Soundarya & Gururaj (2026) document degradation on out-of-distribution content. Key governance implication: effectiveness degrades without ongoing retraining |
| Adversarial robustness | Moderate | Can be made moderately robust through adversarial training at cost of accuracy on non-adversarial content |
| Compute | Moderate | Transformer-based classifiers require GPU compute |
| Real-time | Partial | Feasible on dedicated GPU; not viable for CPU-only deployment |

---

### Multimodal Ensemble
| Criterion | Rating | Source & Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Benchmark accuracy | Highest (~96%) | Singh et al. (2025); Soundarya et al. (2025) consistently report highest performance |
| Generalization | Moderate-High (~74%) | Complementary signal coverage means if one modality's features are out-of-distribution, others may still discriminate |
| Adversarial robustness | Moderate-High | Evading all modalities simultaneously is significantly harder than evading any one |
| Compute | High | Running parallel visual/audio/metadata/linguistic classifiers with fusion is computationally expensive |
| Real-time | No | Singh et al. (2025) explicitly identify this as infeasible at platform scale without dedicated infrastructure |

**Governance implication (§7.2):** The best detection approach is the least deployable. Platform obligations must mandate infrastructure investment to close this gap — it cannot be closed by algorithm selection alone.

---

### Frequency-Domain Masking
| Criterion | Rating | Source & Reasoning |
|---|---|---|
| Benchmark accuracy | High (~88%) | Doloriel et al. (2025) report high accuracy across multiple architectures |
| Generalization | High (~81%) ★ Best | Doloriel et al. (2025) — highest cross-architecture generalization in the corpus. Spectral features are architecture-agnostic: both GAN and diffusion generation leave detectable spectral signatures |
| Adversarial robustness | High | Frequency-domain features are harder to selectively suppress without degrading generation quality — more entangled with signal integrity than spatial features |
| Compute | Moderate | FFT and spectral masking are substantially cheaper than full multimodal ensemble |
| Real-time | Partial | Feasible with GPU acceleration; not viable CPU-only |

**Overall assessment:** The best single method for practical deployment given the generalization crisis. Primary gap: limited large-scale real-world validation. Primary recommendation: prioritize real-world evaluation of this approach for platform mandate consideration.
