# Detection Comparison Notes
## Project Phantom — /detection-analysis

This document explains the methodology behind the ratings in Tables 2 and 4 of the paper and provides source-level justification for each rating. All values should be interpreted as relative assessments derived from the reviewed literature, not as absolute empirical measurements.

---

## Five-Dimension Evaluation Framework

Detection approaches were evaluated across five dimensions adapted from frameworks proposed in the reviewed survey literature (Khan et al., 2025; Gong & Li, 2024; Lin et al., 2025):

### Dimension 1: Benchmark Accuracy
**Definition:** Performance on standard deepfake detection benchmark datasets as reported in the primary literature.  
**Caveat:** As Gong & Li (2024) demonstrate, benchmark datasets are typically constructed under controlled conditions. Benchmark accuracy systematically overstates real-world performance. These figures reflect the best reported results under favorable conditions.  
**Scale:** Very Low (<60%) / Low (60–70%) / Moderate (70–80%) / Moderate-High (80–88%) / High (88–94%) / Highest (>94%)

### Dimension 2: Generalization
**Definition:** Ability to correctly classify deepfakes generated by architectures not represented in the training distribution.  
**Key issue:** The shift from GAN-based to diffusion-based generation (Liu et al., 2025; Soundarya & Gururaj, 2026) represents the primary generalization challenge. A method's generalization score reflects how well it performs specifically on out-of-distribution content.  
**Scale:** Low (<45%) / Moderate (45–68%) / Moderate-High (68–78%) / High (>78%)

### Dimension 3: Adversarial Robustness
**Definition:** Resistance to performance degradation when generative models are specifically optimized to evade the detector (adversarial deepfakes).  
**Source:** Khan et al. (2025) provide the primary framework for adversarial robustness evaluation in this domain.  
**Scale:** Very Low / Low / Low-Moderate / Moderate / Moderate-High / High

### Dimension 4: Computational Cost
**Definition:** Relative compute requirements for inference at scale.  
**Relevance:** Directly determines real-time deployment feasibility. Singh et al. (2025) identify this as the primary barrier to multimodal deployment.  
**Scale:** Very Low / Low / Moderate / High

### Dimension 5: Real-Time Deployment Feasibility
**Definition:** Whether the method can realistically be deployed in a real-time content moderation pipeline at platform scale.  
**Source:** Lin et al. (2025) evaluate real-world deployment feasibility explicitly; their findings inform this dimension.  
**Values:** Yes / Partial / No

## Per-Method Rating Justification

### Visual Artifact Detection
- **Benchmark accuracy (High/~92%):** Well-established across GAN-era benchmarks; multiple papers report >90% accuracy on FaceForensics++ and similar datasets
- **Generalization (Low/~38%):** Guarnera et al. (2023) and Liu et al. (2025) demonstrate that diffusion-generated images lack the spatial/frequency artifacts that these classifiers depend on. Performance on diffusion content approaches chance in several reported evaluations
- **Adversarial robustness (Low):** Trivially bypassed by post-processing (blurring, compression) or by adversarially training the generator against the detector
- **Compute (Low):** Single-pass CNN inference; very low latency
- **Real-time (Yes):** Widely deployed in commercial content moderation pipelines

### Biological Signal Analysis
- **Benchmark accuracy (Moderate/~78%):** Effective against first/second-generation models; Khan et al. (2025) report moderate accuracy across benchmark evaluations
- **Generalization (Moderate/~62%):** More architecture-agnostic than pixel-level approaches since it targets physiological patterns rather than generation artifacts; degrades when high-quality source video is available to the attacker
- **Adversarial robustness (Moderate):** Requires the generative model to explicitly simulate biological signals, which adds cost but is achievable with high-fps training data
- **Compute (Low):** Temporal signal extraction is computationally light
- **Real-time (Yes):** Feasible in video stream analysis

### Audio Spectral Analysis
- **Benchmark accuracy (Moderate-High/~84%):** Effective against earlier voice cloning; Martinek (2025) and Singh et al. (2025) report moderate-high accuracy on benchmark audio
- **Generalization (Moderate/~58%):** Newer architectures (VITS, VALL-E) significantly close the spectral detectability gap, particularly for short clips under 3 seconds
- **Adversarial robustness (Low-Moderate):** Spectral post-processing can partially mask generation artifacts; some robustness when combined with prosody analysis
- **Compute (Low):** FFT-based spectral analysis is computationally efficient
- **Real-time (Yes):** Real-time audio stream analysis is feasible

### Metadata Analysis
- **Benchmark accuracy (Moderate/~71%):** Effective when metadata is present and intact; Lin et al. (2025) note that metadata consistency alone yields moderate discriminative power in controlled settings
- **Generalization (Low/~32%):** Metadata stripping is trivial; any sophisticated attacker removes metadata before distribution. Generalization assumes metadata survives the distribution pipeline, which it frequently does not
- **Adversarial robustness (Very Low):** Zero robustness against an attacker who strips or spoofs metadata — a trivial operation
- **Compute (Very Low):** Reading file headers requires negligible computation
- **Real-time (Yes):** Near-instantaneous; deployed widely as a first-pass filter

### ML-based Classifiers
- **Benchmark accuracy (High/~91%):** State-of-the-art performance on standard benchmarks; Khan et al. (2025) report high accuracy for neural network classifiers on matching-distribution test sets
- **Generalization (Moderate/~66%):** Better than artifact-based methods but limited by training distribution; Soundarya & Gururaj (2026) demonstrate performance degradation on out-of-distribution architectures
- **Adversarial robustness (Moderate):** Can be made moderately robust through adversarial training, but this reduces accuracy on non-adversarial content
- **Compute (Moderate):** Transformer-based classifiers require meaningful inference compute; feasible on GPU infrastructure
- **Real-time (Partial):** Feasible with dedicated GPU; not viable for CPU-only deployments

### Multimodal Ensemble
- **Benchmark accuracy (Highest/~96%):** Consistently highest reported accuracy across all reviewed survey literature; combining modalities eliminates single-modality blind spots
- **Generalization (Moderate-High/~74%):** Better than any single approach due to complementary signal coverage; Singh et al. (2025) confirm consistent superiority
- **Adversarial robustness (Moderate-High):** Evading all modalities simultaneously is significantly harder than evading any single modality
- **Compute (High):** Running parallel visual, audio, metadata, and linguistic classifiers with fusion is computationally expensive
- **Real-time (No):** Singh et al. (2025) explicitly identify computational cost as the barrier to real-time deployment; infeasible at platform scale without dedicated infrastructure investment

### Frequency-Domain Masking
- **Benchmark accuracy (High/~88%):** Doloriel et al. (2025) report high accuracy across multiple generation architectures on benchmark evaluation
- **Generalization (High/~81%):** The highest generalization score of any reviewed method; spectral features are more architecture-agnostic than pixel-level features because generation artifacts in the frequency domain persist across both GAN and diffusion paradigms
- **Adversarial robustness (High):** Frequency-domain features are harder to selectively suppress without degrading generation quality than pixel-level features
- **Compute (Moderate):** FFT and spectral masking require moderate compute; substantially less than full multimodal ensemble
- **Real-time (Partial):** Feasible with GPU acceleration; not viable for real-time CPU-only deployment

## Important Caveats for Readers

1. **All percentage values in Table 4 are approximations** derived from ranges reported across multiple cited benchmark evaluations. They are intended to illustrate relative differences, not assert precise empirical values. Primary sources should be consulted for exact figures.

2. **Benchmark ≠ real-world performance.** Lin et al. (2025) demonstrate consistent and significant degradation when detection models are deployed outside their training distribution. Real-world performance for all methods should be assumed to be lower than benchmark figures.

3. **The ratings reflect the state of the literature as of early 2026.** The generative AI landscape evolves rapidly; a method rated Low generalization today may improve significantly with architectural updates or new training data.
